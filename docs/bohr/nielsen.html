Hans Halvorson Physics, Logic, Philosophy

Rasmus Nielsen

During his lifetime, Rasmus Nielsen (1809-1884) was the most renowned philosopher in the Scandinavian countries. Unfortunately for his philosophical legacy, he was an explicitly Christian philosopher — most interersted the old question of faith vs. reason — at the time that the Scandinavian countries were moving at a breakneck pace in the direction of secularization. In short, the next generation of Scandinavian thinkers thought they had no use for Nielsen’s philosophy, and so it has been almost completely forgotten. (Not a single one of Nielsen’s philosophical works has been translated into English.)

We think, however, that Nielsen had much more of an enduring influence than the subsequent generation realized. In fact, we believe that Rasmus Nielsen is the key figure for understanding Niels Bohr’s relationship to the preceding philosophical tradition — from Descartes through Kant and on to Kierkegaard, etc.. It would have been difficult, in many ways, for Bohr to have been directly influenced by Kierkegaard. After all, Kierkegaard basically expressed distaste for the natural sciences — or to contrast with what we said earlier about Bohr, Kierkegaard did not have a pro-science attitude. But Rasmus Nielsen was interestingly different. In fact, the big mystery about Rasmus Nielsen is how his admiration of Kierkegaard led him to undertake a diligent study of the natural sciences and their methods. (Nielsen’s later works include detailed discussion of recent advances in mathematics, physics, chemistry, biology, and psychology. Nielsen introduced Darwin’s theory of evolution to Denmark, and he argued that it is perfectly compatible with traditional Christian doctrines.) Nielsen was, in an important sense, the first philosopher of science in European history.

Introducing Denmark to Darwin

The faith-reason controversy

As mentioned before, Nielsen — unlike Kierkegaard — was inspired by the natural sciences. He believed that science (Videnskab) is an essential part of the task of human life, and that it delivers great goods to human kind.

Nielsen had been working up to it for many years, and in his book Grundideernes Logik (1864), he states it directly:

Faith and reason are different kinds of things.1

As we can see from his lecture notes, Nielsen had been preaching this lesson to his students for many years hence. However, the publication of the overt statement seems to have occasioned a backlash by young intellectuals, and most notably by Georg Brandes who wrote the pamphlet Dualisme i vores nyeste Philosophie (1866). In this pamphlet, Brandes argues that Nielsen’s conception of the relation between faith and reason does not mesh with human psychology.

Open questions

There is much work still to be done in figuring out what Rasmus Nielsen’s views and arguments were — and evaluating whether there are any good ideas in there. Besides his many published works (all in Danish), Nielsen left handwritten notes that have been preserved by the Royal Library of Denmark. The library also maintains notes written by his students, e.g. Harald Høffding.
