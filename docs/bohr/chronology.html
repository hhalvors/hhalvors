Chronological Overview

1885: Birth of Niels Bohr

  • Date: October 7, 1885
  • Place: Strandgade, Copenhagen, Denmark
  • Niels Henrik David Bohr was born to Christian Bohr, a professor of physiology at the University of Copenhagen, and Ellen Adler Bohr, from a wealthy Danish-Jewish family.
  • Shortly after his birth, the family moved to the medical institute on Bredgade, where Christian Bohr had been appointed professor of physiology.
  • Learn more about Niels Bohr on Wikipedia.

1887: Birth of Harald Bohr

  • Date: April 22, 1887
  • Harald Bohr, Niels’ younger brother, was born. Harald became a notable mathematician and also an accomplished football player, representing Denmark at the 1908 Olympics.

1901: Max Planck Proposes the Quantum of Action

  • Event: Max Planck introduces the concept of the quantum of action, laying the groundwork for quantum theory.
  • This work would later significantly influence Bohr’s own research.
  • Read more about Planck’s quantum theory in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

1905: Einstein’s Annus Mirabilis

  • Einstein’s Photon Hypothesis: Albert Einstein proposes the idea of light quanta (photons), a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics.
  • Special Theory of Relativity: Einstein formulates the special theory of relativity, revolutionizing our understanding of space and time.
  • Both of these developments influenced Bohr’s thinking on atomic structure.

1911: Bohr’s PhD Thesis

  • Death of Christian Bohr: Bohr’s father, Christian Bohr, dies on February 3, 1911, a significant personal loss for Niels.
  • PhD Thesis: Bohr defends his doctoral dissertation (May 13,
    1. titled “Studies on the Electron Theory of Metals” (Metallernes Elektrontheori), at the University of Copenhagen.
  • More on Bohr’s early academic work can be found in this Nature article.

1912: Marriage to Margrethe Nørlund

  • Date: August 1, 1912
  • Bohr marries Margrethe Nørlund, who becomes his lifelong partner and intellectual collaborator. They would have six children together, although two of them, Christian and Harald, died young.

1913: Bohr’s Atomic Model

  • Event: Bohr publishes three landmark papers on the structure of atoms, introducing the Bohr model, which describes electrons orbiting the nucleus in quantized energy levels.
  • These papers were crucial in the development of quantum mechanics and earned him international recognition.
  • Explore Bohr’s model in more detail in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

1916: Birth of Christian Bohr (Son)

  • Date: March 5, 1916
  • Christian Bohr, named after Niels’ father, is born. Tragically, he dies in a boating accident in 1934.

1916: Appointment as Professor

  • Event: Niels Bohr is appointed to a professorship at the University of Copenhagen. This marks the beginning of his leadership in Danish physics and his growing international influence.

1921: Establishment of the Institute for Theoretical Physics

1922: Nobel Prize in Physics

  • Event: Bohr is awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics “for his services in the investigation of the structure of atoms and of the radiation emanating from them.”
  • His work laid the foundation for much of modern atomic theory.
  • Nobel Prize details can be found on the official Nobel Prize website.

1923: Bohr-Kramers-Slater Theory

  • Event: Bohr, along with Hendrik Kramers and John Slater, proposes the Bohr-Kramers-Slater (BKS) theory, suggesting that energy conservation might be violated in individual atomic processes.
  • This theory was eventually refuted, but it played a role in the development of quantum mechanics.

1924: Bohr’s Correspondence Principle

  • Event: Bohr elaborates on his correspondence principle, which states that the behavior of systems described by quantum mechanics replicates classical physics in the limit of large quantum numbers. This principle was key in developing the quantum theory and connecting it with classical mechanics.
  • Heisenberg arrives in Copenhagen

1925: Key Experiments and Heisenberg’s Matrix Mechanics

  • Bothe-Geiger Experiment: Demonstrates that energy is conserved in individual processes, contradicting the BKS theory.
  • Compton-Simon Experiment: Provides further evidence against the BKS theory.
  • Heisenberg’s Matrix Mechanics: Werner Heisenberg publishes his paper on matrix mechanics, a formulation of quantum mechanics.
  • Heisenberg and the development of quantum mechanics are discussed in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

1926: Schrödinger’s Wave Mechanics and Visit to Copenhagen

  • Schrödinger’s Wave Mechanics: Erwin Schrödinger publishes his series of papers on wave mechanics, another formulation of quantum mechanics.
  • October: Schrödinger visits Copenhagen and delivers lectures. During this visit, tensions between Schrödinger and Bohr arose, leading to debates about the interpretation of quantum mechanics.
  • Accounts of this visit suggest that Bohr’s assertive personality may have led to confrontations with Schrödinger.
  • More on the Schrödinger-Bohr relationship

1927: The Concept of Complementarity

  • Winter: Bohr goes on a ski trip to Tretten, Norway, where he develops the idea of complementarity, a cornerstone of his interpretation of quantum mechanics.
  • September: Bohr delivers the famous Como lecture, where he formally introduces the principle of complementarity, emphasizing the dual nature of quantum objects.
  • Explore the principle of complementarity in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

1927: Solvay Conference and Uncertainty Principle

  • Event: The fifth Solvay Conference takes place in October, where Bohr and Heisenberg further develop the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics. It is at this conference that Heisenberg presents his uncertainty principle, which states that certain pairs of physical properties, like position and momentum, cannot both be known to arbitrary precision.
  • More on the Solvay Conference and the uncertainty principle.

1930: Solvay Conference

  • Einstein presents the photon box thought experiment.

1932: Heisenberg in Copenhagen

  • Event: Werner Heisenberg, one of Bohr’s most important collaborators, spends significant time in Copenhagen, where he works closely with Bohr on the interpretation of quantum mechanics. This period is crucial in solidifying the Copenhagen interpretation as a leading view in the physics community.
  • Read about Heisenberg’s work in Copenhagen in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

1933

  • Bohr and Rosenfeld, “Zur Frage der Messbarkeit der elektromagnetischen Feldgrössen”

1935: The EPR Paradox and Bohr’s Response

  • Event: The Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen (EPR) paper is published, challenging the completeness of quantum mechanics.
  • Bohr responds with a defense of quantum mechanics, arguing that the EPR paper misunderstands the nature of measurement and quantum states.
  • Details on the EPR paradox can be found in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

1936: Copenhagen Conference on the Unity of Science

  • Event: In 1936, Bohr hosts an important conference in Copenhagen on the Unity of Science. This meeting was part of the broader international movement known as the Unity of Science movement, which sought to integrate different branches of knowledge into a coherent whole. The conference brought together prominent scientists and philosophers, including Otto Neurath and Karl Popper.
  • The meeting highlighted Bohr’s interest in the philosophical implications of science and his belief in the need for interdisciplinary dialogue.
  • More on the Unity of Science movement can be found in the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

1943: Bohr’s Escape from Nazi-Occupied Denmark

  • Event: During World War II, Bohr, of partial Jewish descent, flees Denmark to avoid Nazi persecution.
  • He escapes to Sweden by boat and eventually makes his way to the United States, where he contributes to the Manhattan Project.
  • Learn more about Bohr’s escape and his role in the Manhattan Project.

1949: Bohr’s “Open Letter to the United Nations”

  • Event: Bohr writes an open letter to the United Nations, advocating for international cooperation on nuclear energy and arms control. He emphasizes the importance of transparency and communication among nations to prevent nuclear conflict.
  • Bohr’s open letter can be explored further on the Nobel Prize website.

1954: The Founding of CERN

  • Event: Bohr plays a key role in the founding of CERN, the European Organization for Nuclear Research, as part of his commitment to international scientific collaboration.
  • [Learn more about Bohr’s involvement in CERN](https://home.cern/about/who-we

1957: Establishment of the International Centre for Theoretical Physics (ICTP)

  • Event: While not directly founding ICTP, Bohr’s legacy and advocacy for international scientific cooperation influenced the establishment of institutions like ICTP. His work inspired many physicists involved in its creation, emphasizing the importance of fostering scientific research in developing countries.
  • Learn more about ICTP.

1958: Bohr Institute’s Anniversary

  • Event: The Niels Bohr Institute at the University of Copenhagen celebrates significant anniversaries, reflecting Bohr’s enduring legacy in the field of physics. The institute continues to be a leading center for research in quantum mechanics and other areas of physics.
  • Bohr remains actively involved in mentoring young physicists and contributing to theoretical physics discussions.

1960: Advocacy for Global Scientific Responsibility

  • Event: Bohr continues to advocate for the responsible use of scientific knowledge, particularly in the context of nuclear energy and weapons. He emphasizes the ethical obligations of scientists to consider the broader implications of their work on humanity.
  • Bohr participates in various international forums and discussions aimed at promoting peace and ethical considerations in scientific advancements.

1962: Niels Bohr’s Passing

  • Date: November 18, 1962
  • Place: Copenhagen, Denmark
  • Niels Bohr dies in Copenhagen at the age of 77.
