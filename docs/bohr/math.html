Hans Halvorson Physics, Logic, Philosophy

Bohr's philosophy of mathematics

When it comes to mathematics, philosophers have divided conceptual space into two main categories, “Platonism” and “nominalism”, where Platonism is, roughly speaking, realism about mathematical objects. The debate between Platonists and nominalists about mathematics has a precursor in the famous medieval debate about universals. For example, besides my gray computer and the gray sky in Copenhagen, does “gray” itself exist?

In the twentieth century, the philosophical debate about mathematics had two main phases. In the first phase, practicing mathematicians tried to find their orientation to recent developments, with some of them adopting a formalist framework, others adopting an intuitionistic framework, and yet others adopting the newly developed set theory of Zermelo and Fraenkel. In the second phase, philosophers — under the influence of W.v.O. Quine — discussed “ontological commitments”, leaving many nominalists (and most notably Quine himself) convinced that good-faith acceptance of the theories of physics demanded acceptance of Platonism about mathematical objects.

Of course, not all philosophers liked being told that the only rational position is Platonism about mathematical objects.1 As a result, there have been great efforts over the past fifty years to reformulate physical theories so that they don’t “quantify over” mathematical objects (see especially (Field 2016)).

At somewhat the opposite extreme, some contemporary philosophers see mathematics as the ideal form of scientific representation — a form of representation that allows us to jump over many of the pitfalls of natural language representation.

Niels Bohr’s view about (applied) mathematics has two main components:

  1. “Mathematics is a precisification [forfinelse] of natural language”.

  2. We are suspended in (natural) language.

Field, Hartry. 2016. Science Without Numbers. Oxford University Press.

  1. Contemporary philosophers seem to be deathly afraid of Platonism? But why? Perhaps they are worried that if they let in abstract objects, then God might be next. A more reasonable worry is that Platonism isn’t a good (scientific) theory about what exists. Or even more reasonably, even if abstract objects “exist”, the kind of existence they have would be very different from the kind of existence enjoyed by familiar physical objects. For example, even if I say that “there is a smallest even number”, I have no idea how to answer the question “how many number 2s are there?”↩︎
