Hans Halvorson Physics, Logic, Philosophy

Poul Martin Møller

While Niels Bohr knew a lot of philosophy, his respect for European philosophers seems to have decreased during his lifetime. We have, in fact, no records of Bohr saying positive things about any European philosophical tradition – neither rationalism, empiricism, Kantianism, nor positivism. There was only one philosopher whom Bohr cited in a positive fashion: the little-known Danish philosopher, Poul Martin Møller, who wrote next to nothing, and who died in 1838.

Introduction

Poul Martin Møller (1794-1838) is remembered primarily for the people he influenced — most notably Søren Kierkegaard (his student) and Niels Bohr (who loved Møller’s novella, En Dansk Students Eventyr). Møller may have had many philosophical ideas, but he didn’t leave behind much of a philosophical literature.

Møller’s early life was fairly typical for the gentry in early nineteenth century Denmark. He was the son of a parish pastor, went to a fancy high school (gymnasium), and then studied theology at the University of Copenhagen. On the personal side, Møller’s life was a bit tumultuous, with at least one very serious infatuation that didn’t lead anywhere. After getting his degree from the University of Copenhagen, Møller became a chaplain on a ship, and while onboard, he translated works of ancient Greek literature into Danish.

Upon returning to Denmark, Møller was called to a post teaching philosophy at the University in Kristiania (now Oslo). Norway had only received independence in 1814, and in the early 1800s, Denmark and Norway had a common academic language. To be more accurate: there were many spoken dialects in Norway, but the written language (bokmål) was Danish.

During his time as professor in Oslo, Møller seems to have fallen under the influence of Hegel. But Møller’s positive interest in Hegel proved to be short-lived. He was offered a professorship at the University of Copenhagen in 1832, and here he joined in with FC Sibbern’s rejection of the Hegelian viewpoint. Recall also that Søren Kierkegaard had entered the University in 1830, and Møller was to become one of his primary influences. (Møller’s residence was in the “new square” (Nytorv), just across from the Kierkegaard residence on the “old square” (Gammeltorv). The Høffding family home was also in Gammeltorv, and a commemorative plaque can still be seen today.)

Works

Møller didn’t publish much in the way of academic philosophy. One notable exception is his article about the possibility of life after death (Jensen and Stewart 2022). He also left behind some lecture notes and some aphorisms that have been collected in various places, including the book Strøtanker.

Møller also wrote poetry and novellas, most notably En Dansk Students Eventyr — a book that contains quite a bit of philosophy.
